Reflections and Conclusion

Lessons Learned

This project taught us the value of inclusive design and user-centered development. We understood that accessibility is not a feature but an integral part of digital product design. Talking to users with color blindness provided us with a glimpse into the everyday difficulties they encounter and highlighted the impact that careful design can have on their web experience. One of our main learnings was how to design with feasible constraints in mind, without the possibility to try out functionality with real-world interaction. This underscored the need for precise visual communication and clear interfaces. We also discovered how important it is to properly depict system feedback in a static or semi-interactive prototype, particularly when you're designing for accessibility. Finally, we discovered how some design decisions—like where a button goes or how well a slider is labeled—can have a huge impact on user understanding.

What Worked Well

Our iterative design process allowed us to respond quickly to feedback, leading to meaningful improvements such as the implementation of color boost sliders and real-time preview filters.

Areas for Improvement

One challenge was ensuring that filter adjustments remained readable and accessible across various screen settings and lighting environments. Future versions could benefit from automated testing tools that simulate different types of color vision. Better use of animation and annotations within the prototype would have helped clarify these elements.

Meeting User Needs

Our prototype serves the needs of color vision deficiency users specifically by allowing them to personalize their online experience. Throughout the installation flow, profile creation and testing, every action was designed with accessibility in mind. Addition of a community-sharing mechanism also promotes collaborative improvement and shared experiences.

Impact of Heuristic Evaluation

The heuristic evaluation process helped identify subtle usability issues, such as a lack of immediate feedback for some settings or inconsistent icon usage. By applying Nielsen’s 10 Usability Heuristics, we refined elements such as system visibility, feedback, and user control. These changes significantly enhanced the clarity and intuitiveness of the final prototype.
